# How to Use Resonance Tuner

Welcome to Resonance Tuner, a premium instrument tuning application designed for high-precision measurement using Autocorrelation and Inharmonicity modeling.

## 1. Getting Started
- **Select Instrument**: Beneath the main gauge, choose between **General Tuner**, **Guitar**, or **Piano**.
- **Grant Permissions**: Click the **Play** button. Your browser will ask for microphone access. You must allow this for the tuner to work.

## 2. Tuning Your Instrument
- **Play a Note**: The app will automatically detect the pitch and display the Note (e.g., A4) and Frequency (Hz).
- **Read the Gauge**:
  - **Blue Needle/Ring**: You are out of tune. 
  - **Left of Center**: The pitch is Flat.
  - **Right of Center**: The pitch is Sharp.
  - **Green Needle/Ring**: You are "In Tune" (within a tight 4-cent tolerance).
- **Deviation**: The large number in the center shows your deviation in **Cents (¢)**. A cent is 1/100th of a semitone.

## 3. Guitar Mode
- Use the **Presets** section to select common tunings like **E Standard**, **Drop D**, or **432 Hz**.
- The tuner is optimized for the quick decay of guitar strings using a "sticky" logic that prevents the display from jumping between notes.

## 4. Piano Mode (Advanced)
Piano tuning is complex because of **Inharmonicity** (string stiffness causes overtones to be sharp).
- **Novice vs. PRO**: Novice uses standard Equal Temperament. PRO uses high-precision stretching.
- **Calibration (Crucial)**: 
  1. Select **Piano** -> **Calibration**.
  2. Click **Play** and play every note on your piano individually, from the lowest bass to the highest treble.
  3. This maps the "speaking length" and stiffness of your specific strings.
  4. Click **Save Piano Profile** when the progress bar reaches 100%.
  5. The tuner will now provide custom target frequencies tailored specifically to make *your* piano sound harmonious.

## 5. Safety Warning
Please read the **Terms of Service** carefully. Tuning a piano involves extreme mechanical tension. If you are not a trained technician, you risk snapping strings or causing structural damage to your instrument.
